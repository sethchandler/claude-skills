# Extended Thinking Moves and Examples

This file provides additional detail on the creative heuristics law professors use, with concrete examples from different areas of law.

## Full Repertoire of Thinking Moves

### 1. Rename the Problem

State the doctrinal puzzle three ways, each foregrounding a different mechanism. Notice how the implied solution changes with the frame.

**Example: Qualified immunity**

- Frame A (information cost): "When should plaintiffs bear the cost of uncertainty about what conduct violates clearly established law?"
  - Implied solution: Shift to defendants who can cheaply obtain legal advice ex ante
- Frame B (error allocation): "Should we prefer false negatives (rights violations without remedy) or false positives (deterring lawful official conduct)?"
  - Implied solution: Depends on relative social costs; empirical question
- Frame C (separation of powers): "Which institution—trial judge, jury, or appellate court—should decide when a right was 'clearly established'?"
  - Implied solution: Reassign from appellate panels to juries with proper instructions

### 2. Level-of-Generality Lever

Restate the holding at three levels and show how outcomes migrate.

**Example: Grutter v. Bollinger (affirmative action)**

- Micro: "Law schools may use race as one factor in admissions to achieve educational benefits of diversity in professional education"
- Meso: "Government actors may use race-conscious classifications when they serve compelling interests in diversity and are narrowly tailored"
- Macro: "The Constitution permits race-conscious government action to remedy present effects of societal discrimination"

Notice: At micro level, only law schools win; at macro level, many race-conscious programs survive. The real fight is over which level controls.

### 3. Anomaly Hunt

Find the case that doesn't fit the line but never got overruled. Explain its survival—procedural posture? remedy? institutional context? Your anomaly often contains the doctrine's real rule.

**Example: In a line of cases restricting government speech regulations**

If every case struck down content-based regulations EXCEPT for one case where the government could ban fighting words in schools, ask:
- Why did that case survive?
- Was it the school context (institutional deference)?
- Was it the remedy sought (injunction vs. damages)?
- Was it the plaintiff (student vs. adult citizen)?

Often the misfit reveals that the doctrine is really about institutional competence, not the stated principle.

### 4. Remedy-First Inversion

Start with the remedy that would improve the system. Reverse-engineer the narrowest rule that justifies it.

**Example: Police misconduct**

- Better remedy: Direct damages against police department budget, not individual officers
- Works back to rule: "Municipalities are liable for constitutional violations when they act with deliberate indifference to risks, as evidenced by repeated similar violations"
- Mechanism: Aligns department incentives with constitutional compliance; reduces individual officer deterrence concerns

### 5. Burden Shift Thought Experiment

Move the burden (production or persuasion) to the other party. Predict litigation behavior over 5 years.

**Example: Employment discrimination**

Current rule: Plaintiff proves prima facie case → Burden of production shifts to employer → Burden of persuasion remains with plaintiff

Alternative: Plaintiff proves prima facie case → Burden of persuasion shifts to employer

Predicted effects:
- More settlements (employers face higher expected costs at trial)
- More summary judgment motions by employers early in discovery
- More employer prophylactic policies to create documentary trail
- Possible perverse effect: Less hiring of protected groups to avoid litigation risk

### 6. Counter-Narrator

Rewrite the fact section from the losing party's strongest perspective. Which "facts" become legal conclusions, and vice versa?

**Example: Miranda v. Arizona (original dissent's view)**

Majority's fact: "Police interrogation was inherently coercive"
→ Dissent's reframe: "Defendant voluntarily confessed after being properly informed of his rights"

Majority's legal conclusion: "Confession was involuntary"
→ Dissent's factual claim: "There was no evidence of physical coercion or psychological pressure"

Notice how factual characterization (was there pressure?) contains the legal conclusion (was confession voluntary?).

### 7. Time-Travel Move

Ask how this dispute would play in different eras. Name the institutional forces driving migration.

**Example: Commercial speech**

- 1890: No protection; advertising is purely commercial conduct
- 1937: No protection; economic regulation gets rational basis
- 1976: *Virginia Pharmacy*—First Amendment applies but lower tier scrutiny
- 1995: High protection; closer to political speech
- 2025: Debate over algorithmic content moderation
- 2030?: Likely question: Can government regulate AI-generated commercial speech?

Institutional forces: Rise of consumer movement (1970s), deregulation era (1980s-90s), digital platforms (2010s), AI (2020s)

### 8. Adjacent-Field Mirror

Borrow one tool from a neighbor field. Show what it reveals—and what it misleads.

**Example: First Amendment public forum doctrine ← Antitrust market definition**

Import: "Market definition" becomes "forum definition"
- Traditional public forum = natural monopoly (streets, parks)
- Designated public forum = market the government chose to enter
- Non-public forum = proprietary activity

Reveals: Government speech restrictions parallel monopolist behavior concerns

Misleads: Antitrust cares about consumer welfare; First Amendment serves democratic self-governance (different ends)

### 9. Cost-of-Information Lens

Reframe the rule as a bet about who can cheaply generate or verify decisive facts.

**Example: Warrantless searches incident to arrest**

Current rule: Police may search person arrested and immediate surroundings

Information cost frame: "Who can more cheaply determine whether a search is reasonable—police in the moment, or judges reviewing after the fact?"

Alternative rule if we thought judges better positioned: Require contemporaneous magistrate approval via phone/video

Reveals: Current rule assumes police are low-cost, low-error decision-makers for this category

### 10. Pathological Hypo, Then Taming

Craft outrageous hypothetical; cut back until plausible. Your real boundary sits one cut earlier.

**Example: Free speech and government employee discipline**

Outrageous: "Can government fire employee for any political speech whatsoever, even off-duty criticism of unrelated policy?"
→ Cut back: "Even if speech is about job-related matter?"
→ Cut back: "Even if speech disrupts workplace operations?"
→ Cut back: "Even if disruption is minimal?"
→ Plausible boundary: "Government can discipline when employee speech, on matters of public concern, substantially disrupts workplace operations"

Your doctrine lives at the "substantially disrupts" line.

### 11. What Would It Take to Reverse?

Draft the paragraph that lets a later court narrow or flip without admitting it.

**Example: Writing as if you're a future court limiting current precedent**

"While *Smith* held that [broad rule], that case involved [distinguishing fact X]. Here, by contrast, [different fact Y] is present, which implicates [different concern Z]. We therefore hold that *Smith*'s reasoning does not extend to this context."

This exercise reveals:
- Which facts in the current case are truly load-bearing
- What limiting principles would be plausible
- Where the doctrine will evolve under pressure

## Classroom Application Examples

### Example 1: Teaching *Pennoyer v. Neff* (Civil Procedure)

**Opening puzzle:** "Why would the Supreme Court care about a land dispute in Oregon?"

**Teachable tension:** "Courts must assert power over defendants, but that power must be limited by something—geography? consent? fairness? What constrains a court's reach?"

**Three one-fact deltas:**
1. What if Neff had been personally served in Oregon? (In-state service)
2. What if the property was in California, not Oregon? (Forum vs. property location)
3. What if Neff had hired a lawyer who appeared? (Consent vs. coercion)

**Counter-narrator moment:** "Defend Oregon's position: Oregon courts SHOULD be able to adjudicate title to Oregon land regardless of where the defendant lives. Why would that be a workable rule?"

**Exit bridge:** "If you think this case makes personal jurisdiction too complicated, you now have a paper: 'Simplifying Personal Jurisdiction: A Proposal for Property-Based Rules in the Digital Age.'"

### Example 2: Teaching *Wickard v. Filburn* (Constitutional Law)

**Opening puzzle:** "Can Congress regulate wheat you grow for your own chickens?"

**Teachable tension:** "The Commerce Clause gives Congress power over interstate commerce. When does intrastate activity have enough connection to interstate commerce to justify federal regulation?"

**Three one-fact deltas:**
1. What if Filburn sold his excess wheat locally? (Local commercial vs. personal consumption)
2. What if only one farmer did this? (Aggregation principle)
3. What if Filburn grew marijuana for personal use? (*Raich* preview)

**Counter-narrator moment:** "Defend Filburn: If Congress can regulate this, what CAN'T it regulate? Make the most aggressive version of this argument."

**Exit bridge:** "Next class we'll see how *Lopez* tried to walk back *Wickard*—and why that's harder than it looks."

## Scholarship Application Examples

### Example 1: Paper Seed on Qualified Immunity

**Claim that moves a case:**
"Courts should deny qualified immunity at summary judgment when the defendant officer's conduct violated a right that was clearly established by a Supreme Court case, even if no prior case involved identical facts. This would reverse the result in cases like *Kisela v. Hughes* where courts granted immunity despite clear Supreme Court precedent on excessive force."

**Mechanism:**
Reduces two types of error: (1) False negatives (victims without remedy despite rights violations), (2) Signal error (police don't know which conduct is prohibited because courts keep finding new factual distinctions). Trades judicial economy (fewer immunity determinations) for deterrence (officers face liability more often).

**First adopter + clean limit:**
- First adopter: District courts can apply this rule when denying summary judgment motions
- Clean limit: Only applies when plaintiff can cite Supreme Court case; circuit splits and district court cases remain insufficient for clearly established law

**Strongest counter-abstract:**
"This proposal would expose officers to unpredictable liability by requiring them to analogize from Supreme Court cases addressing different factual scenarios. Officers need bright-line rules for split-second decisions. Making immunity turn on Supreme Court cases alone ignores that circuits develop law to address fact patterns the Court hasn't reached. The current system properly balances individual rights against the need for officers to act without fear of personal liability for reasonable mistakes."

**Research vectors:**
1. Empirical: Study qualified immunity grants/denials rates across circuits
2. Comparative: How do other countries handle police liability?
3. Doctrinal: Trace "clearly established" standard from *Harlow* through present
4. Adjacent field: Explore how administrative law handles "arbitrary and capricious" review with unclear standards

### Example 2: Paper Seed on Major Questions Doctrine

**Claim that moves a case:**
"The major questions doctrine should be reconceived as a burden-shifting rule rather than a substantive limitation on agency authority. When an agency claims authority to decide a question of 'vast economic and political significance,' the burden should shift to the agency to show clear congressional delegation. This would preserve agencies' ability to address major issues while ensuring congressional intent, and would reverse results in cases like *West Virginia v. EPA* only where the statutory language is genuinely ambiguous."

**Mechanism:**
Preserves institutional competence (agencies handle complex technical issues) while enforcing accountability (Congress must clearly authorize major policies). Reduces judicial error by forcing courts to engage with statutory text before reaching constitutional avoidance.

**First adopter + clean limit:**
- First adopter: District courts reviewing agency action can apply this framework to *Chevron* step one analysis
- Clean limit: Doctrine should not apply when statute contains explicit delegation to address the category of problem at issue (e.g., "regulate air pollutants endangering public health")

**Strongest counter-abstract:**
"This proposal would weaken the major questions doctrine into ordinary *Chevron* analysis, losing the doctrine's core insight that Congress doesn't hide elephants in mouseholes. Administrative agencies routinely overreach by claiming broad authority from general statutory language. Requiring 'clear' delegation is not a burden-shifting rule; it's the correct reading of the Constitution's nondelegation principle. The proposal also gives agencies a roadmap to evade the doctrine by manufacturing 'clear' statements through creative statutory reading."

**Research vectors:**
1. Doctrinal: Trace major questions from *Brown & Williamson* through *West Virginia v. EPA*
2. Empirical: How often do agencies win/lose under major questions vs. standard *Chevron*?
3. Comparative: Study parliamentary systems where delegation defaults differ
4. Constitutional: Engage with nondelegation literature (Posner & Vermeule, Hamburger)
5. Adjacent field: How does securities law handle delegation of authority to SROs?

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Theme-heavy, mechanism-light**: Claiming a rule promotes "fairness" or "efficiency" without explaining the actual causal chain

2. **Slogan worship**: Saying courts should apply "heightened scrutiny" without specifying burden allocation, standard of proof, or institutional capacity

3. **Universal claims**: Asserting a principle works everywhere without identifying the contexts where it breaks

4. **Ignoring downstream effects**: Proposing a rule change without predicting how lawyers, agencies, and lower courts would respond

5. **Bibliography without direction**: Citing 50 sources without explaining which ones answer which questions

6. **Forgetting institutional constraints**: Proposing solutions that require constitutional amendments, unanimous Supreme Court agreement, or perfect information

## Quick Self-Check Questions

Before finalizing any analysis, ask:

- **For teaching materials**: Would a smart student who got this "wrong" feel like they learned something, or just feel corrected?
- **For scholarship**: Can you name the motion and remedy your thesis would change in a real case?
- **For mechanisms**: Could a skeptical lawyer explain to a client how your proposal would work in practice?
- **For boundaries**: Can you state one context where your idea should lose—and defend why that loss is good?
- **For research**: If a research assistant returned tomorrow with your suggested sources, would you know what questions to ask them?
